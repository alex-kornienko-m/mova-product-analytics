# mova-product-analytics
Product analytics case study for MOVA (EdTech AI app) evaluating user acquisition across geographies, analyzing paywall A/B testing variations, and modeling funnel unit economics.

# **Task 1: Justification for the Selected Testing Topic**

## **Selected Topic: "Real Practice Without Fear" (Human-like AI Language Partner)**

**Lead phrase:** "Tired of robotic AI, but too shy to practice with real people?"

After analyzing Reddit, Instagram, and YouTube, we identified three of the most active audience pain points — which are essentially one cluster:

- **I have no one to practice with**
- **Not expressing myself the same way as in my native language**
- **Language apps are useless**
![Imahe ](assets/comments.png)

**Core Insight**

People want to practice with someone real, but are terrified of being judged by actual people. And conventional language apps feel too lifeless to serve as a substitute. MOVA fills this gap: a human-like AI partner that sounds like a real person but never judges you for your mistakes.

### **1\. Why is this topic broad enough to scale?**

- **Global nature of the problem:** The fear of speaking a foreign language is the classic "intermediate plateau" that most language learners worldwide encounter. A person may already understand written text or video, but faces a psychological barrier when it comes to live conversation.
- **Combining 3 pain points into one concept:** Since the topic covers not a single narrow request (e.g., "English for travel") but a fundamental communication need, the market potential is enormous. This enables campaigns to scale across different geographies and audience segments without rapidly exhausting the audience.

### **2\. Who is the target audience and what is their specific pain?**

- **Target audience:** "Passive Knowers" — people with a basic or even solid theoretical foundation (they studied the language at school, university, or through apps) but lack conversational practice.
- **Specific pain (The Psychological Paradox): Core Insight:** People crave conversation with someone real, but are panicked by the fear of judgment or criticism from actual people (due to a low proficiency level, poor pronunciation, or the long mental search for the right words).
- **Pain related to existing solutions:** Traditional language apps (Duolingo, Babbel, etc.) feel too "dead" and useless for conversational progress — they deliver dry grammar and vocabulary but fail to create the feeling of a live conversation.

### **3\. Why can this topic work in paid Meta traffic?**

- **Complete market vacuum (Competitive Analysis):** In June, we analyzed 1,073 Meta Ads in the language learning niche and found not a single ad that directly addressed this audience paradox (fatigue with robots + fear of people). This gives us a unique competitive advantage and an unoccupied positioning niche. Examples:

_LearnaAI - shows only problems with usage or how easy it is to use ai._

_Ewa - don’t say smth about ai._

_Speals AI - doesn’t say that people are tired of ai, only shows advantages of it._

- **High emotional trigger for Meta algorithms:** Recognition shame is the strongest stop-scroll trigger, because a person is not just interested, they stop and think “this is about me”. This generates long first views (3+ sec) and comments like “omg same” - both signals are interpreted by Meta as high-quality engagement and increase organic sharing. None of the direct competitors (LearnAI, Ewa, Speals AI) activate this emotion - they either show the benefits of the product or avoid the topic of AI altogether. This means that the auction for this audience is not occupied, and the CPM will be lower with a higher CTR.

## **Strategic Positioning for MOVA**

At first glance, the topic seems paradoxical: it builds on the pain of 'I'm tired of AI, I need a real person,' while the product itself is AI. This is not a bug — it is our USP. People are not tired of AI itself; they are tired of robotic AI.

We don't argue with the objection "I need a real person" — we resolve it: MOVA sounds so natural (3,000 hours of real conversations, 500 native speakers from various regions and dialects) that the audience's objection becomes our strongest argument — "you can't tell it apart from a human."

We must position MOVA as a Human-like AI Language Partner. This is not just another dull AI chatbot, but:

- A safe conversation partner that sounds completely natural.
- A companion that helps you practice real-life dialogues.
- A space free from judgment or shame for making mistakes.

Main message: There's no need to wait for the perfect speaking partner or for the moment when you feel ready. MOVA is human-like AI practice without judgment: natural enough to train real dialogues, and safe enough to not be afraid of mistakes.

**CTA:** Start speaking before you're ready. MOVA will get you there.

### **Step 2 — Two Strongest Concepts**

**Concept #5**

**Selection rationale:**

We selected Concept #5 because it targets a behavioral moment that is both universal and highly specific: the late-night motivation spike. This is not a niche situation — it affects virtually every self-improvement learner regardless of geography or language goal. The hook works because it captures a real internal conflict: the user is finally ready, but every available option (tutor, friend, native speaker, app) fails them at that exact moment. This makes the frustration immediate and personal, which drives strong scroll-stop behavior. Unlike Concepts #1 and #2, which rely on repeated trial-and-error scenes that can feel slow in the first 3 seconds, Concept #5 builds tension through a fast sequence of blocked options — each one adding to the emotional pressure before the resolution. This structure is better suited for short-form video formats (Reels, Stories) where attention is won or lost in under 2 seconds.

**Concept #6**

**Selection rationale:**

We chose this creative because it operates not at the level of an individual user pain point, but at the level of how the user perceives the entire category of language learning AI. The market's problem right now is not that people lack practice or motivation — there are already far too many such messages and they have faded into the background. The primary barrier with cold audiences in the US is different: most AI language apps sound and feel unnatural, too AI-like, and therefore don't create the effect of a real conversation. As a result, even good products lose attention before the user even tries them in depth.

This creative enters directly at this layer of doubt and flips it. It doesn't convince the person that 'you need to practice,' it calls into question the quality of existing AI solutions: what if the core problem is not with you or the learning, but with how AI looks and sounds? This is intended to create a cognitive conflict in the first few seconds and compel the viewer to keep watching, because the user wants to verify whether this is truly different from everything they've seen before.

This approach is better suited for performance marketing because it doesn't rely on specific situations or emotional scenes — other concepts often hinge on fear, shame, or individual life moments, making them harder to scale and more prone to 'creative burnout' in tests. Here, the foundation is far simpler: does this AI sound like a human? Because of this, dialogues, examples, and proof points can be easily changed without altering the core idea.

**Why didn't we choose the earlier concepts?**

We eliminated the earlier creative concepts not because they lacked emotional or narrative strength, but because they failed to establish a distinct market position for MOVA. Those concepts relied on common industry tropes and universal pain points, which ultimately categorized us as just another practice app. Our selected direction shifts the core criteria from the user asking whether they need to learn a language to the user questioning whether our AI sounds genuinely human.

Regarding the specific concepts, we compared the café embarrassment scenario and the 10 PM motivation scenario. While both address social fear and lack of practice opportunities, they operate differently. The café concept relies on a specific social setting, which creates strong resonance but limits the audience to those who have experienced that exact situation. By contrast, the 10 PM motivation concept captures a universal internal state that any learner can relate to, making it significantly easier to scale across diverse audience segments without relying on specific past memories.

We ultimately chose concepts five and six as a complementary pair for testing. Concept five targets users who already possess the desire to practice but lack the opportunity, effectively selling availability and readiness. Concept six targets users who remain skeptical of AI language tools by selling credibility and technical differentiation. Together, these two concepts address distinct psychological barriers within the cold audience funnel, moving the viewer from the need for practice to the conviction that MOVA is the superior solution.

# **Task 2.1: User Acquisition Management - GEO Analysis**

## Recommended market for scaling: United States (US)

Based on aggregated data across four geographic regions (US, UK, CA, AU), we analyzed user acquisition efficiency and monetization performance. The United States has been selected as the priority geography for scaling in the next quarter.

### **1\. Economic Efficiency (LTV/CAC)**

The US market delivers the strongest return on marketing investment with an LTV/CAC ratio of 1.96x — the highest across all four markets. Despite carrying the highest CPM ($52.40), the monetization quality of the US audience fully offsets this cost.

At first glance, the US looks expensive: CPM of $52.40 is 53% higher than the UK's $34.20, which means fewer impressions for the same budget. However, when we run the full funnel at a $20,000 weekly budget, the US generates an estimated $8,611 in revenue versus $6,272 for the UK — a 37% advantage. The higher CPM is fully offset by superior LTV ($18.40 vs $11.20) and stronger funnel conversion. Paying more per impression is justified when each acquired user is worth significantly more over 6 months.

### **2\. Product Metrics and Engagement**

The quality of the US audience is confirmed by the strongest retention and activity metrics across all markets:

- **Day-7 Retention: 38%** — 9 percentage points ahead of AU (29%) and 4 points ahead of CA (34%)
- **Avg Sessions/Week: 4.2** — the highest across all geos, indicating users are actively building a habit around the product

High session frequency is a direct driver of long-term LTV growth. The US shows a stable core of users who not only install the app but return to it consistently — a critical signal for sustainable scaling.

### **3\. Sales Funnel Efficiency**

The US leads in conversion across every funnel stage:

- **CR Install → Trial: 22%**
- **CR Trial → Pay: 34%**
- **Overall CR Install → Pay: 7.48%** — 1.18 percentage points ahead of CA (6.3%), the nearest competitor

This means that for every 1,000 installs, the US converts 75 more users to paying customers than CA. At scale, this gap compounds significantly.

# **Task 3.1:** A/B Test Analysis

You have run an A/B test of two paywall variants. The test ran for 4 weeks, with traffic split 50/50 between the variants. Both variants consist of a single screen, with no onboarding.

### **Task**

- Determine the winning variant. Justify your choice through specific metrics — not LTV alone.
- Explain why the metrics differ so significantly between variants. What user behavior does this reflect?
- Propose 2 hypotheses for the next test: what else could be tested on the paywall or email page to improve the performance of the winning variant?

| **METRIC** | **VARIANT A** | **VARIANT B** |
| :--- | :---: | :---: |
| Price | $9.99/week | $19.99/month |
| Offer Framing | "7 days free, then $9.99/week" | "Start free, then $19.99/month" |
| Paywall Style | Single screen, 1 plan | Single screen, 1 plan |
| CR Paywall → Trial | 41.0% | 28.0% |
| CR Trial → Pay | 29.0% | 44.0% |
| CR Paywall → Pay | 11.89% | 12.32% |
| Avg Revenue / Paid User (Day 30) | $26.5 | $19.6 |
| Cancel Rate (Day 30) | 68.0% | 41.0% |
| LTV (6 months) | $48 | $45 |
| Refund Rate | 3.2% | 1.8% |

## Winning Variant: B ($19.99/month)

#### **1\. Determining the Winner**

The winning variant is **Variant B**. The decision is based on four metrics evaluated together, not LTV alone.

**CR Paywall → Pay: 12.32% vs 11.89%  
**The most important single metric in this test. It measures how efficiently each variant converts raw traffic into paying customers. Variant B wins despite having a lower CR Paywall→Trial (28% vs 41%), because the quality of users who start the trial is significantly higher — 44% of them convert to paid vs only 29% in Variant A. From the same volume of traffic, Variant B produces more actual paying customers.

**Refund Rate: 1.8% vs 3.2%  
**Variant A's refund rate is 78% higher than Variant B's. This is not just a revenue leak — a refund rate above 3% triggers App Store flags, risks payment processor penalties, and generates negative reviews that increase CAC for all future campaigns. Variant B's 1.8% is within a healthy range and creates no systemic risk.

**Cancel Rate (Day 30): 41% vs 68%  
**This is the starkest difference in the entire dataset. In Variant A, 68% of paying users cancel within 30 days — meaning nearly 7 out of 10 users who paid once will not generate any further revenue. In Variant B, 59% of paying users remain active past Day 30, creating a compounding revenue base as campaigns scale.

**Revenue per 1,000 paywall visitors:**

- Variant A: 1,000 × 11.89% × $48 = **$5,707**
- Variant B: 1,000 × 12.32% × $45 = **$5,544**

Variant A shows a marginal $163 advantage here. However, at a $20,000 weekly budget this gap is negligible and is fully offset by Variant B's structural advantages in retention and refund rate.

**Why not Variant A despite higher LTV ($48 vs $45)?**

CAC is identical for both variants — traffic was split 50/50 from the same budget, so acquisition costs do not differ. The $3 LTV gap in favor of Variant A (6.7%) does not change the conclusion because:

1.  The revenue-per-1,000-visitors difference is only $163 — operationally insignificant at this scale.
2.  Variant A's 3.2% refund rate creates real downstream costs not captured in LTV: chargeback fees, support overhead, and App Store reputation risk.
3.  Variant A's 68% cancel rate means the higher LTV is driven by front-loaded weekly charges, not genuine long-term retention — making it fragile and difficult to scale safely.

#### **2\. Why Do the Metrics Differ? Understanding User Behavior**

The data reveals that the two variants are attracting fundamentally different types of users.

**Variant A attracts impulsive starters.  
**The $9.99/week price point feels low-commitment — the barrier to starting a trial is minimal. Users opt in easily (41% CR Paywall→Trial) because the perceived risk is small. However, when the first real charge arrives, many realize they are effectively paying ~$40/month, which they never consciously agreed to. This disconnect drives the 68% cancel rate, 3.2% refund rate, and explains why Day 30 revenue ($26.5) front-loads most of the 6-month LTV ($48) — the majority of value is extracted in the first 2–3 weekly billing cycles before churn.

**Variant B attracts committed users.  
**The $19.99/month framing sets a clear and honest expectation upfront. The higher perceived price filters out users who are not genuinely interested — only 28% start the trial. But those who do are already pre-sold on the value: 44% convert to paid, only 41% cancel by Day 30, and the refund rate stays at 1.8%. These users understand what they are paying for and are more likely to remain active past the first billing cycle, which is why Variant B's revenue curve is more gradual but more stable than Variant A's.

**One counterintuitive signal worth addressing:  
**Variant A generates higher Avg Revenue per Paid User at Day 30 ($26.5 vs $19.6). This happens because weekly billing front-loads revenue — users are charged immediately and repeatedly in the first weeks. However, this advantage disappears by month 6: LTV converges to $48 vs $45, a difference of only 6.7%. The reason is the 68% cancel rate — most Variant A users churn after 2–3 weekly charges, meaning the early revenue spike does not compound. Variant B generates less in month 1 but retains users longer, producing a more stable and predictable revenue curve that is better suited for scaling.

#### **3\. Two Hypotheses for the Next Test**

**Hypothesis 1: Adding a "How Your Trial Works" Timeline Block**

The primary friction point in Variant B is its low CR Paywall→Trial (28% vs Variant A's 41%). A significant portion of users who see the $19.99 price drop off before starting the trial — likely due to fear of unexpected charges or uncertainty about when billing begins.

Proposed change: Add a visual timeline below the CTA button showing exactly how the trial works: _"Today: you pay nothing → Day 5: we'll remind you your trial ends in 2 days → Day 7: you become a Premium member at $19.99/month. Cancel anytime before Day 7."_

Expected outcome: Reducing billing anxiety should increase CR Paywall→Trial from 28% toward 33–35%, while preserving the audience quality that makes Variant B's Trial→Pay conversion strong. If CR Paywall→Trial reaches 33% with Trial→Pay held at 44%, overall CR Paywall→Pay improves from 12.32% to 14.52% — a 18% uplift in paying users from the same traffic.

**Hypothesis 2: Introducing an Annual Plan as a Decoy**

Currently Variant B presents a single plan ($19.99/month). Users evaluate this price in isolation, which means some will drop off simply because they have no reference point for whether it represents good value.

Proposed change: Add a second plan — an annual subscription at $119.99/year ($10/month equivalent) — displayed alongside the monthly plan. The annual plan acts as a Decoy: its primary function is not to drive annual conversions, but to reframe the monthly plan as the rational, affordable choice. Against a $119.99 anchor, $19.99/month feels like a low-risk, easy decision.

Expected outcome: The monthly plan conversion rate increases as users shift from evaluating absolute price ("is $19.99 worth it?") to comparing options ("the annual plan saves money long-term but $19.99/month is safer to start"). Overall CR Paywall→Pay is expected to grow by 15–20% based on established anchoring effect benchmarks in subscription paywalls. A small percentage of users may also choose the annual plan, directly increasing LTV for those users to ~$120 vs the current $45.

## **3\.** Conclusion: Does Unit Economics Work?

**Yes — at current metrics, unit economics is positive.**

With Variant B's CR Trial→Pay (44%) applied to the US funnel, each paying user generates a contribution margin of **+$15.93**. The current LTV ($45.00) covers CAC ($29.07) at a ratio of **1.55x** — above the 1.5x threshold considered healthy for early-stage scaling.

This is a direct result of combining two strengths:

- **US market quality:** strong funnel conversion (CR Install→Trial: 22%) and high LTV driven by engagement (Day-7 Retention: 38%, Avg Sessions/Week: 4.2)
- **Variant B paywall:** high-quality user selection (CR Trial→Pay: 44%) with low churn (Cancel Rate: 41%) and low refund rate (1.8%)

Neither input alone is sufficient. US market data with Variant A's CR Trial→Pay (34%) would yield only 531 paying users, CAC of $37.66, and a contribution margin of **+$7.34** — still positive, but significantly weaker. Variant B is what makes economics work comfortably.

## 4\. Conclusion: Does Unit Economics Work?

**Recommended action: Improve CR Trial→Pay from 44% to 55% through in-trial engagement optimization.**

The current break-even point requires LTV ≥ $29.07. We are already above it. However, increasing CR Trial→Pay is the highest-leverage lever available because it simultaneously reduces CAC and increases the paying user base without touching the ad budget.

**Justification through numbers:**

If CR Trial→Pay improves from 44% to 55%:

| **Metric** | **Current (44%)** | **Improved (55%)** |
| :--- | :---: | :---: |
| Paying Users | 688 | 860 |
| CAC | $29.07 | $23.26 |
| LTV | $45.00 | $45.00 |
| Contribution Margin | +$15.93 | +$21.74 |
| Total Weekly Profit | $10,960 | $18,697 |

A 25% relative increase in CR Trial→Pay produces a **71% increase in weekly contribution margin** — without increasing the ad budget by a single dollar.

**How to achieve this:** Focus on days 3–6 of the trial, when the conversion decision is made. Specific interventions:

- Push notification on Day 3 with a personalized progress summary ("You've already completed 4 conversations — here's what you've improved")
- In-app milestone at Day 5 showing a before/after fluency comparison

These are product-side changes with zero marginal cost that directly move the most impactful metric in the funnel.
